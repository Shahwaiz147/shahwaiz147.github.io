Every story of university life has a base of operations, and for me, that base was Room 120 in Ali Hall. After spending the first forty-eight hours processing the external shock of the campus, I finally had to sit down and truly confront the four walls that would enclose my private life. Ali Hall itself is an old building, bearing the scars of countless batches of engineering students who had carved their names into its history before moving on. The corridors were long, echoing chambers where every slammed door sounded like a gunshot and the hum of old ceiling fans provided a constant, low-frequency soundtrack to our daily anxieties. Room 120 sat somewhere in the middle of this concrete labyrinth, a small rectangular space that felt less like a bedroom and more like a minimalist holding cell when I first stepped inside.

The room contained the bare essentials required to keep a student functioning, but absolutely nothing more. There were two iron bed frames with thin, state-issued mattresses that had seen far better days, two basic wooden study tables with straight-backed chairs that practically guaranteed back pain after an hour of reading, and two built-in wall lockers made of dark, heavy metal. The walls were painted an uninspiring shade of off-white, peeling slightly near the ceiling where past roof leaks had left their mark. My roommate hadn't arrived yet, so the emptiness of the space magnified every little flaw. The window opened up to a view of the inner courtyard, allowing the hot afternoon breeze to blow in, bringing along the faint scent of dust and distant cafeteria prep. It was a space entirely devoid of comfort, waiting for someone to drag it into liveability.

The first few hours were spent in a slow, therapeutic battle against the dust. I bought a cheap cloth, a plastic bucket, and some detergent from the small utility shop near the hostel gate and went to work. Scrubbing the wooden desk, wiping down the metal edges of the bed, and sweeping out the corners of the room became my way of taking control of an environment that felt completely alien to me. As I worked, I looked closely at the desk surface. It was covered in deep scratches, ink stains, and faded inscriptions left by previous occupants—scribbled formulas from old calculus exams, names of hometowns, and half-erased tallies counting down the days to graduation. Seeing those marks changed something inside me. I wasn't the first person to sit in this room feeling lonely and overwhelmed, and I wouldn't be the last. This room had seen hundreds of students arrive in anxiety and leave as engineers.

By the evening, Room 120 had transformed, if not into a home, then at least into a functional sanctuary. I threw a clean bedsheet over the mattress, arranged my Python and physics textbooks on the shelf, and set up my laptop on the desk. When I finally turned off the main fluorescent tube light and switched on my small desk lamp, the harsh, institutional feeling of the room softened into something manageable. The shadows hid the peeling paint, and the small circle of warm light on my desk made the space feel focused and private. Sitting there, listening to the chaotic shouts of guys playing corridor cricket outside my door, I realized that Room 120 was exactly what I made of it. It was a blank, rugged slate—a small, protected pocket of reality where I could retreat, study, and figure out who I was supposed to become in this vast, quiet university.
