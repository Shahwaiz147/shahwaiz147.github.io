**Ali Hall Room 120: My New Home Away from Home**

When I first arrived at UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus, one of the biggest changes in my life was moving into the hostel. For many students, university is not only about attending classes and studying new subjects. It is also about learning how to live independently. Since I came from a different city, I was allotted a room in Ali Hall, specifically Room 120. At that time, I did not realize that this room would become an important part of my university journey and would teach me lessons beyond academics.

Before joining the university, I had imagined hostel life differently. I expected a comfortable room, a well-organized environment, and a place where everything would be managed efficiently. Like many new students, I had created a picture in my mind based on stories from friends, social media posts, and my own expectations. However, reality turned out to be somewhat different from what I had imagined.

The first time I entered Room 120, I immediately understood that hostel life would require adjustment. The room was functional, but it was clear that the hostel building was old and had been in use for many years. Some parts of the room needed maintenance, and the overall condition was not exactly what I had expected. At first, this was disappointing because I was already adjusting to a completely new environment. Everything around me was unfamiliar, and the condition of the room added another challenge to the transition.

During those early days, I spent a significant amount of time arranging my belongings and trying to make the room feel more comfortable. Moving away from home is not easy, especially when it is your first experience living independently. At home, everything is familiar and easily accessible. In a hostel, even simple tasks require planning and adjustment. I had to organize my study materials, clothes, daily necessities, and schedule in a way that would allow me to live efficiently in a limited space.

One of the most important things I learned during this period was that comfort does not always come from the physical condition of a place. Initially, I focused on what the room lacked. Over time, however, I began focusing on how I could adapt and make the best use of what was available. This shift in perspective helped me appreciate hostel life more than I had during the first few days.

Ali Hall itself had a unique atmosphere. It was home to students from different departments, cities, and backgrounds. Every room contained individuals with their own goals, challenges, and stories. Walking through the corridors, it was common to see students discussing assignments, preparing for quizzes, or simply sharing their experiences of university life. Although everyone was busy with their own academic responsibilities, there was still a sense of community among hostel residents.

One aspect of hostel life that stood out to me was the importance of independence. At home, many daily responsibilities are handled by family members. In the hostel, students must manage their own routines. Whether it was waking up on time, maintaining the room, organizing study schedules, or handling everyday challenges, everything depended on personal responsibility. Initially, this felt overwhelming, but it gradually became one of the most valuable parts of the experience.

Another challenge involved maintenance-related issues. Since the building was older, certain problems occasionally appeared. One of the most memorable examples occurred during rainy weather. Whenever heavy rain arrived, water would sometimes enter the room, creating inconvenience and forcing us to find temporary solutions. At the time, these situations were frustrating, but looking back, they became memorable experiences that added character to hostel life. Years later, I will probably remember these challenges more clearly than many ordinary days.

The hostel administration also faced its own limitations. Sometimes issues required longer than expected to be resolved. As a new student, I initially found this frustrating because I was accustomed to quicker responses in previous environments. However, university life taught me that not every problem can be solved immediately. Learning patience became an important part of my personal development. Instead of waiting for ideal conditions, I learned to focus on adapting and moving forward.

Despite these challenges, Ali Hall provided something valuable that I could not have gained elsewhere: exposure to diverse experiences and perspectives. Every student had a different story. Some were adjusting to hostel life for the first time, while others had already developed strategies for managing university challenges. Informal conversations often provided practical advice that could not be found in any student handbook. Through these interactions, I learned that university education extends beyond classrooms and textbooks.

Hostel life also helped me understand the importance of time management. Since there was no family structure reminding me about schedules and responsibilities, I had to become more disciplined. Balancing classes, assignments, study sessions, meals, and personal activities required careful planning. While I was not perfect at managing my time during the early months, the hostel environment encouraged me to gradually improve.

The room itself eventually became more than just a place to sleep. It became a space where I reflected on daily experiences, prepared for classes, completed assignments, and planned future goals. Some days were productive and rewarding, while others were challenging and stressful. Regardless of the situation, Room 120 remained a constant part of my university life.

Looking back now, I realize that my initial disappointment with the hostel environment prevented me from seeing its positive aspects. I focused too much on physical conditions and not enough on personal growth. Over time, I learned that growth often happens in environments that are less than perfect. Challenges force us to adapt, and adaptation leads to development. If everything had been comfortable and easy from the beginning, I might not have learned many of the lessons that hostel life taught me.

Another important realization was that university memories are not created only by academic achievements. They are also created by everyday experiences. The conversations in hostel corridors, the late-night discussions with fellow students, the efforts to solve unexpected problems, and the process of adjusting to independent living all become meaningful parts of the journey. These experiences contribute to personal development just as much as formal education.

Today, when I think about Ali Hall Room 120, I do not remember only the maintenance issues, the old building, or the challenges of hostel life. Instead, I remember it as the place where I learned how to live independently, adapt to new circumstances, and take responsibility for my own routine. It was not a perfect environment, but it played an important role in shaping my university experience.

For many students, the hostel is their first real step toward independence. For me, Ali Hall Room 120 served exactly that purpose. It introduced me to the realities of university life and taught me lessons that extended far beyond academics. While my journey at UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus involved many classrooms, laboratories, and courses, some of the most valuable lessons began in a simple hostel room that gradually became my home away from home.
