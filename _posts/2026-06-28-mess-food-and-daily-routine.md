**🍽️ Hostel Mess and Daily Routine Experience**

Life in Ali Hall hostel gradually settled into a fixed daily routine that revolved around classes, hostel timings, and most importantly, the mess schedule. When I first joined university on 15 September 2025, this routine felt completely new and somewhat difficult to adjust to. Coming from a home environment where everything was flexible and comfortable, adapting to a strict schedule required time and patience.

The day usually started early in the morning. Unlike home, where waking up depended on personal choice, hostel life had its own reasons to wake up early. Sometimes it was the noise from other students, sometimes the pressure of morning classes, and sometimes simply the uncomfortable sleep environment that made long rest difficult. Slowly, waking up early became part of the routine rather than a challenge.

After waking up, the next step was getting ready quickly for the day. Everyone in the hostel followed a similar pattern in the morning. There was always a sense of urgency because missing breakfast or being late for class could disturb the entire schedule. This created a habit of time management even without realizing it. I began to understand that hostel life teaches discipline in a very practical way.

Breakfast in the mess was the first major interaction of the day with hostel food. Students would gather at the mess during fixed timings, and the environment was usually half-silent because most people were still waking up mentally. The food in breakfast was simple and repetitive. It was not something students looked forward to, but it became a necessary part of the routine. Sometimes it was paratha with tea, sometimes a very basic meal that served only the purpose of filling the stomach. Despite the lack of variety, breakfast became a consistent starting point of the day.

After breakfast, students would head towards their classes. The walk from hostel to academic blocks slowly became familiar over time. In the beginning, everything felt new and slightly confusing, but gradually the routes, buildings, and timings became part of memory. Morning classes required focus, and after the early routine, students would spend most of their day attending lectures and labs. The thought of returning to hostel and having lunch often kept students going through long sessions.

Lunch was another fixed and important part of hostel life. By the time lunch arrived, students were usually tired from morning classes. The mess would become crowded, and everyone would rush to complete their meal within limited time. Lunch was often a topic of discussion among students, not because of enjoyment but because of its consistency and predictability. The food quality remained average, and students adjusted to it over time rather than expecting change. The environment during lunch was also social, as students shared their class experiences and discussed assignments or upcoming tasks.

After lunch, students would return to their classes or sometimes come back to the hostel if there was a break. The afternoon period was usually the most exhausting part of the day. Concentration levels dropped, especially after long lectures or lab sessions. Despite that, students continued their routine because there was no alternative but to follow the schedule.

Evening time brought a slight change in atmosphere. After returning from classes, students would rest for a short time before preparing for dinner. Some would study, while others would sit with friends or use their phones to relax. This was the only flexible part of the day where students had some control over their time. However, even this time eventually revolved around the mess schedule.

Dinner in the mess marked the final structured meal of the day. Similar to breakfast and lunch, dinner had fixed timings and a predictable menu pattern. Students would gather once again, and the environment was slightly more relaxed compared to lunchtime. Conversations during dinner were more casual, often about random topics, daily experiences, or complaints about classes. Even though the food quality remained consistent in its limitations, dinner became part of the routine that everyone followed without exception.

After dinner, students would return to their rooms. This was the time when the day slowed down. Some students would start studying assignments or preparing for upcoming lectures, while others would simply rest after a long day. Studying in the hostel environment required adjustment because of distractions and shared spaces. Concentration was not always easy, but gradually students learned how to manage their time and focus despite the surroundings.

As days passed, this routine became a natural part of life. What initially felt difficult slowly turned into habit. Waking up early, attending classes, eating at fixed mess timings, and returning to study or rest became a cycle that repeated every day. Hostel life is often not about comfort but about adaptation, and this routine taught exactly that.

Looking back at those early days, the mess and daily schedule played a major role in shaping my university experience. Even though the food was simple and the routine was strict, it helped build discipline and consistency in daily life. Over time, I stopped noticing the discomfort and started following the rhythm of hostel life naturally.
