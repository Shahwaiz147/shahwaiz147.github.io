By the end of my first week in Ali Hall, the romanticized illusions of university independence had completely dissolved, replaced by the rigid, unyielding gears of the hostel daily routine. The defining anchor of this routine—and arguably the ultimate test of physical endurance for any hostelite—was the mess food. Every morning began with the harsh clanging of giant aluminum ladles against metal cooking pots, a sound that resonated through the corridors like a wake-up siren. Dinner was no different; at exactly 7:30 PM, a long, weary line of hungry students wrapped around the dark corridor leading to the basement dining hall, all of us carrying plastic tokens we had purchased earlier in the day, waiting to see what the kitchen staff had engineered for our survival.

To call the meals in Ali Hall a culinary experience would be a massive overstatement. It was purely functional fuel, stripped of flavor, presentation, and warmth. The menu followed a repetitive weekly cycle that never deviated: watery dal that seemed to have more fluid dynamics than nutritional value, pale chicken curry where finding an actual piece of meat required a statistical miracle, and roti that was often so tough it felt like an exercise in jaw mechanics. Sitting at the long, grease-stained wooden benches with hundreds of other guys, eating under the dim flicker of buzzing tube lights, the atmosphere was subdued. There were no grand conversations about high-level technology or engineering innovation here; there was only the quiet, collective chewing of people who were eating simply because they had to survive another day of classes.

This survival baseline quickly dictated the structure of my entire daily routine. I found myself calculating my day not by the clock, but by the availability of tolerable food options. If the afternoon mess meal looked too uninspiring, I would ration a small packet of biscuits or save up a few rupees to buy a plain, dry samosa from the small tuck shop near the main gate. The routine became mechanical. I would wake up at 7:00 AM, scramble through the shared communal bathrooms where hot water was an urban legend, rush to the academic blocks for lectures, and return to the hostel just in time to catch the tail end of the lunch hours. The gaps between classes were no longer filled with exploring the campus; they were spent resting on my thin mattress, preserving energy, and waiting for the heat of the day to subside.

Adjusting to this lifestyle was an exercise in swallowing my pride and lowering my standards of comfort. Back home, a meal was a social, comforting event; here, it was a transactional necessity completed in fifteen minutes flat. Yet, as the days bled into one another, I noticed a subtle change in the way I viewed this harsh routine. The complaints about the poor food quality, while still constant, became a common language that bonded the freshers together. We laughed about the mysterious consistency of the gravy, shared hidden stashes of homemade pickles sent by our mothers, and lent each other extra tokens when someone ran out of cash before the weekend. The daily grind of Ali Hall was stripping away our softness, forcing us to adapt to a rugged reality where luxury didn't exist, but shared resilience did.
